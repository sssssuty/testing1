Project Prompt
How might MSA develop a method to help improve and monitor and track safety training efficiency and efficacy?

A solution that would include seamless integration with the Connected Work Platform, as well as vision, strategy, business model, and execution plan.
Deconstructing the Prompt

The team started the project by breaking down the prompt into smaller questions to better understand the problem space. The questions included:

  • What products or services does MSA Safety provide?
  • What role does MSA play in safety training?
  • How does the oil and gas industry currently conduct and monitor safety training?
  • How does the industry currently track efficiency and effectiveness?
  • What is the Connected Work Platform?

About MSA Safety

Founded in 1914, MSA is a global leader in safety products for personal and facility protection. Serving clients in over 140 countries, MSA provides both hardware and software solutions across various industries, including oil & gas, utilities, healthcare, construction, fire service, mining, and manufacturing.

For this project, we are focusing on MSA's Connected Work Platform, which integrates the ALTAIR io4 portable gas detector with The Grid software. This combination enhances worker visibility and safety, streamlines compliance, and boosts the productivity of gas detection programs. The io4 Portable Gas Detector, designed for use in confined spaces, collects crucial data such as gas readings, calibration status, and bump test results.
The Oil and Gas Industry

The oil and gas sector is immensely profitable, generating $4 trillion in 2022 alone, with a consistent daily profit of $2.8 billion over the past 50 years. Product development in this area embodies the famous quote of “Don’t dig for gold, sell shovels.”

The safety conditions in the oil and gas sector have greatly improved, but gaps remain. The U.S. Safety and Hazard Investigation Board (CSB) noted that the lack of regulations for onshore oil and gas operations contributed to past incidents, especially compared to offshore regulations. As a result, CSB recommended that OSHA require a performance-based safety management system for the industry. A safety manager reflected, "Twenty years ago, workers were seen as replaceable. My goal now is to minimize their risk and ensure they return home safely." He is now supported by flame-resistant clothing, PPE, and improved procedures from his employer, Falcon Drilling.

Incident reporting, while not a tool for immediate prevention, plays a critical role in safety management. It systematically records safety events, allowing for analysis that can reveal trends, identify areas for improvement, and enhance overall safety protocols. According to the Heinrich triangle theory, for every serious injury or fatality, there are 300 near misses. This underscores the importance of capturing data beyond OSHA requirements, as repeated near misses can eventually lead to severe incidents.
Ground Workers

The working and living conditions for these workers are challenging—they spend two weeks every month on-site, working 12-hour shifts each day, and sleep in small steel cabins at the edge of the site. Despite the demanding nature of the job, the pay is substantial, with annual earnings exceeding $80,000. However, the combination of hard work and high pay leads to a high turnover rate, which increases costs for companies due to the need for rehiring and retraining new employees.

We conducted user research sessions and field research to dive deeper into what's on the workers' minds.


Insights include but not limited to:

#1. Workers carries multiple PPEs and devices on them. They don't want to carry more.

#2. Workers' voices might be ignored by the management.

#3. Workers might not report incidents because of scared of reprecussion, lack of oversights or misguided perception.
Quote from a Falcon Drilling worker:

"I care about safety because I want to go home to my family safe and sound. I can see the effort our safety manager spent on keeping us safe, which I appreciate."
Safety Managers/Operation Managers

Depending on the site's size, there may be both safety managers and an operations manager, or just safety managers. Most of them live on-site alongside the workers. As their titles suggest, they are responsible for everything that happens on-site.


Insights include but not limited to:

#1. Safety managers are overwhelmed by the amount of data they have to pay attention to.

#2. Safety hardwares and softwares are purchased from different companies to fit the unique needs of the site and the team.

Solution
Leveraging data to improve worksite safety

Safety sites are equipped with protocols, procedures that outline rules and documentation requirements, and safety equipment that monitors work conditions to ensure safe working levels. These contain vast sources of valuable data that currently sit in “data islands”.

Our solution leverages these data sources and worker input to create a holistic overview of the safety status on the worksite and identify trends and patterns that may shed light on seemingly minor aberrations that may need to be brought to the safety manager’s attention.


Value Propositions:

#1 Coaching for Safety Manager

With intuitive features and comprehensive reporting, it offers a clear understanding of historical data, facilitating informed decision-making and strategic planning. Our solution transforms incident data into a roadmap for improving safety practices, fostering a culture of vigilance and continuous improvement.

#2 Snapshot of Safety Status

Our solution not only addresses major incidents but also sheds light on subtle trends in occurrences, particularly concerning minor events like near misses and unsafe acts. By uncovering these insights, safety managers can address potential risks before they escalate, fostering a safer and more resilient work environment.

#3 Leveraging Data from Device

Device-generated data offers real-time accuracy at the time of the event, providing a reliable source for understanding and evaluating incidents. By incorporating device-generated data, safety managers gain a more comprehensive understanding of events, enhancing their ability to implement targeted preventive measures and improve overall worksite safety.

#4 Personalization

Our proposed solution offers a personalized snapshot of safety status tailored to each worksite. Recognizing that every safety incident is essentially a deviation from site-specific protocols, our machine learning model refines its understanding over time through reported data. This customized approach provides safety managers with nuanced insights that align closely with the operational realities of their site, enabling targeted interventions and fostering a culture of safety excellence.

For ground workers:
Enabling Accurate and Immediate Incident Reporting and Improved Communication
SafetyNet is designed to be user-friendly, reducing the cognitive load on employees. This ensures that workers can report safety issues in the shortest period of time, without distraction from their core duties. SafetyNet also enables direct communication which allows for immediate clarification and discussion, which is crucial in dynamic work environments where conditions can change rapidly.

For Safety Managers:
A Snapshot of Events at the Site
The manager requires a status update on the site, particularly focusing on negative near misses that can be addressed early. The overview provides a high-level view not only of near misses but also of positive observations logged by workers.

Recently logged events, along with relevant device data, are immediately visible to the manager, serving as a starting point for investigation. Identifying frequently occurring events is crucial, as they may be on the verge of escalating into accidents or fatalities.

For Safety Managers:
Trends and Possible Consequences of Events
Data derived from previous root causes along with current incident data can help to form correlations between the both to suggest the manager some corrective actions that could potentially address the near miss.

In cases where any node in the decision tree cannot be supported by data available, that will be highlighted as unverified information. The manager could reach out to a worker to find the missing information, keeping the channel of communication between the stakeholders open.
